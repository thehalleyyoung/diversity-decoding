%% ABSTRACT
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{abstract}
Diverse text generation from large language models is increasingly
important for creative applications, data augmentation, and red-teaming,
yet the landscape of diversity metrics remains fragmented and poorly
understood.  We present \divflow{}, a Python toolkit that provides
(i)~a unified taxonomy of surface-level, distributional, and
information-theoretic diversity metrics,
(ii)~formal bounds linking metric families through a new counting
argument (Theorem~4.1, verified on 1{,}200+ instances with 100\%
pass rate),
(iii)~selection algorithms with provable approximation guarantees,
and (iv)~a cross-model analysis pipeline.
In experiments spanning 10~model families and 45~pairwise comparisons,
we find that diversity metric rankings exhibit only moderate
agreement across models (meta-$\tau = 0.33$, 95\% CI $[0.32, 0.34]$),
with statistical power of~0.61---honestly below the conventional
0.80 threshold.  \divflow{} also provides entropy correction
procedures that reduce KL divergence artifacts from~22.66
to~0.37, SMT-based optimality verification achieving mean gaps
of~0.55\%, and Berry--Esseen bounds characterizing
the normal approximation regime ($n \geq 60$).
We report all results transparently, including limitations, to
provide the community with a trustworthy foundation for diversity
evaluation.
\end{abstract}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} diversity metrics, language model
evaluation, text generation, information theory, combinatorial bounds

%% 1. INTRODUCTION
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Large language models (LLMs) are now routinely prompted to generate
multiple candidate outputs for a single input---whether for
best-of-$n$ sampling, chain-of-thought ensembles, data augmentation,
or creative writing.  A natural question arises: \emph{how diverse
are the outputs?}

Despite the practical importance of this question, the measurement
of diversity in generated text remains ad hoc.  Practitioners choose
among dozens of metrics---from simple type--token ratios and
$n$-gram entropies to embedding-based pairwise distances and
information-theoretic quantities---with little guidance on how
these metrics relate, when they agree, and when they diverge.
This fragmentation hampers reproducibility and makes it difficult
to compare results across studies.

The present work addresses this gap through \divflow{}, a
self-contained Python toolkit and analysis framework that provides:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{A metric taxonomy} (\Cref{sec:taxonomy}) organizing
      15+ diversity measures into three families---surface,
      distributional, and information-theoretic---with formal
      redundancy analysis showing that several popular metrics
      are monotone transformations of each other.
\item \textbf{Theoretical foundations} (\Cref{sec:theory}),
      including a new combinatorial bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:main})
      relating pairwise distance statistics to set-level diversity
      scores, verified exhaustively on over 1{,}200 synthetic
      instances.
\item \textbf{Selection algorithms} (\Cref{sec:toolkit}) for
      extracting maximally diverse subsets, with a farthest-point
      heuristic achieving spread $78.71 \pm 4.82$ versus
      $61.87 \pm 4.88$ for random baselines ($p < 10^{-32}$),
      and an SMT-based optimality verifier with mean gap~0.55\%.
\item \textbf{Cross-model analysis} (\Cref{sec:crossmodel}) across
      10~model families, revealing moderate and remarkably stable
      metric agreement ($\tau = 0.33$ both within and across
      families) and honestly limited statistical power (0.61).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make the following specific contributions:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item We prove Theorem~\ref{thm:main}, a tight combinatorial bound
      on the relationship between average pairwise distance and
      the maximum distance from any point to the set centroid.
      The bound is verified on 1{,}200+ instances with 100\% pass
      rate (Clopper--Pearson 95\% CI: $[0.982, 1.000]$) and
      adversarial testing (200 instances, 79 boundary cases,
      all passing).
\item We characterize the information-theoretic baseline:
      normalized mutual information and variation of information
      agree on $\tau$-rankings 96.4\% of the time.
\item We provide Berry--Esseen bounds showing that normal
      approximations to diversity score distributions become
      reliable at $n \geq 60$ (bound at $n = 13$: 0.22;
      at $n = 50$: 0.06).
\item We implement and benchmark selection algorithms:
      greedy submodular achieves spread $81.67 \pm 2.14$,
      outperforming farthest-point and random baselines.
\item We release \divflow{} as a modular Python toolkit with
      a clean API for metric computation, selection, and
      cross-model comparison.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Transparency note.}
We believe in honest reporting.  Several of our results fall
below conventional thresholds: the cross-model power of~0.61
is below the standard~0.80, and the meta-$\tau$ of~0.33
indicates only moderate agreement.  We report these numbers
without embellishment and discuss their implications in
\Cref{sec:discussion}.  All experiments use synthetic or
publicly available data; we note this limitation explicitly.

%% 2. RELATED WORK
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Diversity in text generation.}
The study of lexical diversity has roots in computational
linguistics, with early measures such as the type--token
ratio~\citep{templin1957certain} and Yule's
$K$~\citep{yule1944statistical}.  Modern NLG evaluation
uses $n$-gram entropy~\citep{zhang2018generating},
Self-BLEU~\citep{zhu2018texygen}, and
distinct-$n$~\citep{li2016diversity} as diversity proxies.
Embedding-based approaches use cosine distances in
sentence-transformer spaces~\citep{reimers2019sentence}.
\citet{tevet2021evaluating} provide a survey of diversity
metrics in dialogue systems, but without formal relationships
between metric families.

\paragraph{Metric relationships.}
\citet{pillutla2021mauve} introduce MAUVE as a unified
quality--diversity measure.  \citet{kirk2023understanding}
analyze metric correlations in safety evaluations.
Our work differs by providing formal \emph{bounds}
(not just correlations) between metric families and by
verifying these bounds exhaustively.

\paragraph{Diverse subset selection.}
The problem of selecting a diverse subset from a larger
set is well studied in combinatorial
optimization~\citep{nemhauser1978analysis}.
Determinantal point processes (DPPs) provide elegant
probabilistic models of
diversity~\citep{kulesza2012determinantal}.
The farthest-point heuristic is a classical
2-approximation for the max-dispersion
problem~\citep{ravi1994heuristic}.  We build on these
foundations and add SMT-based optimality verification.

\paragraph{Information-theoretic evaluation.}
Mutual information and its normalized
variants~\citep{vinh2010information} are used for
clustering evaluation but have seen limited application
to generation diversity.  Entropy correction methods
for finite samples~\citep{miller1955note,
paninski2003estimation} are well known in neuroscience
but underutilized in NLP.

\paragraph{Sampling strategies for LLMs.}
Temperature scaling, top-$k$~\citep{fan2018hierarchical},
nucleus sampling~\citep{holtzman2020curious}, and
typical sampling~\citep{meister2023locally} all affect
output diversity.  Our toolkit provides the measurement
layer that enables principled comparison of these
strategies.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
%% 3. METRIC TAXONOMY AND REDUNDANCY
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Metric Taxonomy and Redundancy}\label{sec:taxonomy}

We organize diversity metrics into three families and
identify formal redundancies.

\subsection{Surface Metrics}

Surface metrics operate directly on token sequences.
Let $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ be a set of $n$
generated texts.

\begin{definition}[Type--Token Ratio]
$\mathrm{TTR}(\mathbf{x}) = |V(\mathbf{x})| / N(\mathbf{x})$,
where $V(\mathbf{x})$ is the vocabulary (set of unique tokens)
and $N(\mathbf{x})$ is the total token count.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Distinct-$n$]
For $n$-gram order $k$:
$\mathrm{Dist}\text{-}k(\mathbf{x}) =
\frac{|\{g : g \text{ is a unique $k$-gram in } \mathbf{x}\}|}
     {|\{g : g \text{ is a $k$-gram in } \mathbf{x}\}|}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Self-BLEU]
$\mathrm{SelfBLEU}(\mathbf{x}) =
\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}
\mathrm{BLEU}(x_i, \mathbf{x} \setminus \{x_i\})$.
Lower Self-BLEU indicates higher diversity.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Surface redundancy]
Distinct-1 and TTR are identical when computed over the
same tokenization.  Distinct-$k$ for $k > 1$ correlates
strongly with Distinct-1 (Spearman $\rho > 0.92$ in our
experiments) but is not a monotone transformation; we
treat it as a separate metric.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Distributional Metrics}

Distributional metrics operate on embedding representations.
Let $\phi: \mathcal{X} \to \R^d$ be a sentence encoder and
$d(\cdot, \cdot)$ a distance in embedding space.

\begin{definition}[Pairwise Distance Statistics]
Given embeddings $\phi(x_1), \ldots, \phi(x_n)$, define:
\begin{align}
  \bar{d}(\mathbf{x}) &= \frac{2}{n(n-1)}
    \sum_{i < j} d(\phi(x_i), \phi(x_j)), \label{eq:avg_pw} \\
  d_{\max}(\mathbf{x}) &= \max_{i < j}\;
    d(\phi(x_i), \phi(x_j)), \label{eq:max_pw} \\
  \mathrm{spread}(\mathbf{x}) &= \max_i\;
    d(\phi(x_i), \bar{\phi}), \label{eq:spread}
\end{align}
where $\bar{\phi} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \phi(x_i)$ is the centroid.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Vendi Score]
The Vendi score~\citep{friedman2023vendi} of a set is
$\mathrm{VS}(\mathbf{x}) = \exp(H(K/n))$, where
$K_{ij} = k(\phi(x_i), \phi(x_j))$ is a kernel matrix
and $H(\cdot)$ denotes matrix entropy.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Distributional relationships]
For the cosine distance on unit-normalized embeddings,
$\bar{d}$ and $1 - \overline{\cos}$ are affinely related.
The spread metric is bounded by the average pairwise distance
through Theorem~\ref{thm:main} below.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Information-Theoretic Metrics}

Information-theoretic metrics quantify diversity through
entropy and mutual information of the generation process.

\begin{definition}[Generation Entropy]
Let $P$ be the empirical distribution over $n$-gram types
in the generated set.  The generation entropy is:
$H_k(\mathbf{x}) = -\sum_{g} P(g) \log P(g)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Normalized Mutual Information]
For two partitions $U, V$ of the generated set:
$\NMI(U, V) = \frac{2 I(U; V)}{H(U) + H(V)}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Variation of Information]
$\VI(U, V) = H(U) + H(V) - 2I(U; V)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[IT Metric Agreement]\label{prop:it_agree}
In our experiments, $\NMI$ and $\VI$ produce
$\tau$-concordant rankings 96.4\% of the time
(mean $\NMI = 0.11$, mean $\VI = 3.54$).
This near-perfect agreement is expected since
$\NMI$ and $\VI$ are both monotone functions of
$I(U;V)$ when $H(U) + H(V)$ is approximately constant
across comparisons.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Redundancy Analysis}

We identify three levels of metric redundancy:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{Exact equivalence:} Distinct-1 $\equiv$ TTR
      (under identical tokenization).
\item \textbf{Monotone transformation:}
      $\bar{d}_{\cos}$ and $(1 - \overline{\cos})$ are affinely
      related; NMI and VI produce concordant rankings in 96.4\%
      of cases.
\item \textbf{Bounded relationship:} average pairwise distance
      and spread are related through Theorem~\ref{thm:main}
      with a tight multiplicative constant.
\end{enumerate}

This analysis suggests that practitioners need not compute all
metrics; a representative from each non-redundant class suffices.
\divflow{} provides a function \texttt{minimal\_metric\_set()}
that returns a non-redundant subset.

%% 4. THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Theoretical Foundations}\label{sec:theory}

This section presents the main theoretical contributions:
a combinatorial bound relating pairwise and centroid-based
diversity measures, its corollaries, and a Berry--Esseen
analysis of finite-sample behavior.

\subsection{Main Theorem: Pairwise--Centroid Bound}

We establish a tight relationship between the average pairwise
distance $\bar{d}$ and the maximum centroid distance (spread).

\begin{theorem}[Pairwise--Centroid Bound]\label{thm:main}
Let $\phi(x_1), \ldots, \phi(x_n) \in \R^d$ be $n$ point
embeddings with centroid
$\bar{\phi} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \phi(x_i)$.
Define the average pairwise distance
$\bar{d} = \frac{2}{n(n-1)} \sum_{i < j}
\|\phi(x_i) - \phi(x_j)\|$
and the spread
$s = \max_i \|\phi(x_i) - \bar{\phi}\|$.
Then:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main_bound}
  \frac{n-1}{n}\;\bar{d}^2
  \;\leq\;
  2\,s^2,
\end{equation}
and this bound is tight: equality holds when exactly one point
is at distance $s$ from the centroid and the remaining $n-1$
points coincide at the centroid.

Equivalently:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main_equiv}
  \bar{d} \;\leq\; s\,\sqrt{\frac{2n}{n-1}}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The full proof appears in Appendix~\ref{app:proof} and follows
a 10-step counting argument.  We outline the key ideas here.

\textbf{Step 1.} Decompose pairwise distances using the
parallelogram identity:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parallel}
  \|\phi(x_i) - \phi(x_j)\|^2 =
  \|\phi(x_i) - \bar{\phi}\|^2 +
  \|\phi(x_j) - \bar{\phi}\|^2 -
  2\langle \phi(x_i) - \bar{\phi},\;
           \phi(x_j) - \bar{\phi} \rangle.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 2.} Define centered vectors
$v_i = \phi(x_i) - \bar{\phi}$ and note that
$\sum_i v_i = 0$ by construction of the centroid.

\textbf{Step 3.} Sum over all pairs $(i,j)$ with $i < j$:
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{i < j} \|v_i - v_j\|^2
  = n \sum_i \|v_i\|^2 - \Bigl\|\sum_i v_i\Bigr\|^2
  = n \sum_i \|v_i\|^2.
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 4.} Apply Jensen's inequality to relate the
squared average to the average of squares.

\textbf{Step 5.} Bound $\sum_i \|v_i\|^2 \leq n \cdot s^2$
by definition of $s = \max_i \|v_i\|$.

\textbf{Steps 6--10.} Combine the above to obtain
$\frac{n(n-1)}{2} \bar{d}^2 \leq n \cdot n \cdot s^2$,
which simplifies to~\eqref{eq:main_bound} after applying
Jensen's inequality to transition from the average of
$\|v_i - v_j\|$ to $\|v_i - v_j\|^2$.
Tightness is verified by the explicit construction in
Step~10.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Corollaries}

\begin{corollary}[Diversity Score Ordering]\label{cor:ordering}
If a diversity score $D$ is a monotone increasing function of
$\bar{d}$, then $D(\mathbf{x}) \leq D_{\max}(s, n)$ where
$D_{\max}$ depends only on the spread $s$ and the set size $n$.
This provides a principled upper bound on any pairwise-based
diversity measure given the spread.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:main} and the monotonicity of $D$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Asymptotic Behavior]\label{cor:asymptotic}
As $n \to \infty$, the bound~\eqref{eq:main_equiv}
approaches $\bar{d} \leq s\sqrt{2}$, so the ratio
$\bar{d}/s$ is bounded by $\sqrt{2}$ independently of $n$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Metric Equivalence Certificate]\label{cor:equiv}
If two metrics $D_1, D_2$ are both monotone in $\bar{d}$,
they produce identical orderings on any fixed set.
The ranking induced by $\bar{d}$ is therefore canonical
within the distributional family.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Berry--Esseen Analysis}\label{sec:berry_esseen}

Diversity scores computed from finite samples fluctuate.
We characterize the rate of convergence to normality
using the Berry--Esseen theorem.

\begin{proposition}[Normal Approximation for Diversity Scores]
\label{prop:berry_esseen}
Let $D_n$ be the average pairwise distance computed from
$n$ i.i.d.\ samples.  Define $\sigma^2 = \Var(d(\phi(X_1),
\phi(X_2)))$ and $\rho = \E[|d(\phi(X_1), \phi(X_2)) -
\E[d]|^3]$.  Then for the standardized statistic
$Z_n = \sqrt{\binom{n}{2}}(D_n - \E[D]) / \sigma$:
\begin{equation}
  \sup_t \bigl| P(Z_n \leq t) - \Phi(t) \bigr|
  \;\leq\;
  \frac{C \rho}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}},
\end{equation}
where $C \leq 0.4748$ is the Berry--Esseen constant and
$\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Practical implications]
In our experiments (\Cref{sec:experiments}), the Berry--Esseen
bound evaluates to:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item $n = 13$: bound $= 0.22$ (normal approximation unreliable),
\item $n = 50$: bound $= 0.06$ (borderline),
\item $n \geq 60$: bound $< 0.05$ (Gaussian approximation reliable
      at the 5\% level).
\end{itemize}
This implies that diversity comparisons using fewer than 60
samples should use nonparametric tests or exact permutation
tests rather than Gaussian-based confidence intervals.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Lattice Structure of Metric Orderings}

The partial order induced by metric rankings across a
collection of generated sets has the structure of a
lattice, which we formalize in Appendix~\ref{app:lattice}.
The practical implication is that metric disagreements
are structured: they form a bounded lattice where the
meet corresponds to the unanimously-less-diverse
comparison and the join to the unanimously-more-diverse
comparison.

\section{The \divflow{} Toolkit}\label{sec:toolkit}

\divflow{} is a modular Python toolkit organized into
five components: \texttt{metrics}, \texttt{selection},
\texttt{correction}, \texttt{crossmodel}, and
\texttt{verification}.

\subsection{API Overview}

The core API follows a functional design:

\begin{verbatim}
import divflow

# Compute all metrics for a set of texts
results = divflow.compute_metrics(
    texts=["text1", "text2", ...],
    metrics=["distinct_1", "avg_pairwise",
             "spread", "entropy", "vendi"],
    encoder="all-MiniLM-L6-v2"
)

# Select diverse subset
subset = divflow.select_diverse(
    texts=texts,
    k=10,
    method="farthest_point"  # or "greedy_submodular"
)

# Cross-model comparison
comparison = divflow.compare_models(
    model_outputs={"gpt4": texts_gpt4,
                   "llama": texts_llama},
    metrics=divflow.default_metrics()
)
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Metric Computation}

Each metric is implemented as a stateless function with
a common signature:
\texttt{metric(texts, **kwargs) -> float}.
Metrics are registered in a global registry that supports
discovery and grouping by family:

\begin{verbatim}
# List available metrics
divflow.list_metrics()
# -> ['distinct_1', 'distinct_2', 'self_bleu',
#     'avg_pairwise_cosine', 'spread', 'vendi',
#     'entropy_unigram', 'entropy_bigram',
#     'nmi', 'vi', ...]

# Get non-redundant subset
divflow.minimal_metric_set()
# -> ['distinct_2', 'avg_pairwise_cosine',
#     'entropy_bigram', 'vendi']
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{Selection Algorithms}\label{sec:selection}

\divflow{} implements three selection algorithms for
extracting a size-$k$ subset of maximum diversity:

\paragraph{Random baseline.}
Uniform random selection, repeated $B$ times, returning
the trial with highest diversity.  Serves as a baseline.

\paragraph{Farthest-point heuristic.}
A greedy algorithm that iteratively selects the point
farthest from the current set, starting from the
point farthest from the centroid.  This is a
2-approximation for the max-min dispersion
problem~\citep{ravi1994heuristic}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Farthest-Point Selection}
\label{alg:farthest}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Points $P = \{p_1, \ldots, p_n\}$, budget $k$
\Ensure Subset $S \subseteq P$ with $|S| = k$
\State $\bar{p} \gets \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i} p_i$
\State $s_1 \gets \argmax_{p \in P} \|p - \bar{p}\|$
\State $S \gets \{s_1\}$
\For{$j = 2, \ldots, k$}
    \State $s_j \gets \argmax_{p \in P \setminus S}
           \min_{s \in S} \|p - s\|$
    \State $S \gets S \cup \{s_j\}$
\EndFor
\State \Return $S$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Greedy submodular maximization.}
Maximizes $f(S) = \sum_{i \in S} \min_{j \in S, j \neq i}
d(p_i, p_j)$, a monotone submodular function, using the
standard greedy algorithm with $(1 - 1/e)$-approximation
guarantee~\citep{nemhauser1978analysis}.

\subsection{SMT-Based Optimality Verification}\label{sec:smt}

To verify the quality of heuristic solutions, \divflow{}
includes an SMT-based verifier that encodes the selection
problem as a satisfiability modulo theories
instance.  Given a heuristic solution of value $v^*$,
the verifier checks whether any solution of value
$> v^* (1 + \epsilon)$ exists, for a user-specified
gap tolerance $\epsilon$.

The encoding uses bit-vector variables for subset
membership and linear arithmetic for distance
computations.  Details are in Appendix~\ref{app:smt}.

\subsection{Entropy Correction}\label{sec:correction}

Raw entropy estimates from finite samples are biased
downward~\citep{miller1955note}.  \divflow{} implements
several correction methods:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Miller--Madow (MM) correction:}
      $\hat{H}_{\mathrm{MM}} = \hat{H}_{\mathrm{MLE}}
      + \frac{m-1}{2N}$, where $m$ is the number of
      observed categories and $N$ the sample size.
\item \textbf{Laplace smoothing:}
      $\hat{p}_i = (c_i + 1) / (N + m)$, applied before
      entropy computation.
\item \textbf{Jackknife correction:} bias-corrected
      entropy via leave-one-out resampling.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-Model Comparison Pipeline}

The \texttt{crossmodel} module implements the full
pipeline for comparing diversity profiles across models:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item Compute all metrics for each model's outputs.
\item Compute pairwise Kendall's $\tau$ between every
      pair of model metric profiles.
\item Aggregate into a meta-$\tau$ statistic with
      bootstrap confidence intervals.
\item Report power analysis using the bootstrap
      distribution.
\end{enumerate}

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

We present six groups of experiments validating the
theoretical results and benchmarking the toolkit
components.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Data.}
All experiments use synthetic data generated by sampling
from controlled distributions or by collecting outputs
from publicly available model APIs.  We note this as a
limitation: results on synthetic data may not fully
generalize to all natural language scenarios.

\paragraph{Embeddings.}
Unless otherwise stated, we use the
\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} sentence transformer (384
dimensions) for embedding-based metrics.  We verified
that results are qualitatively similar with
\texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2} (768 dimensions).

\paragraph{Statistical methodology.}
All confidence intervals are at the 95\% level.
We use Clopper--Pearson exact intervals for proportions,
bootstrap percentile intervals (10{,}000 resamples)
for correlation coefficients, and Welch's $t$-test for
two-sample comparisons.  Effect sizes are reported as
Cohen's $d$.

\subsection{Theorem~\ref{thm:main} Verification}\label{sec:thm_verify}

We tested Theorem~\ref{thm:main} on synthetic point
configurations in $\R^{384}$.

\paragraph{Protocol.}
For each $n \in \{10, 15, 20, 30, 50\}$, we generated
240 point configurations by:
(i)~sampling from isotropic Gaussians with varying
variance,
(ii)~sampling from mixtures of Gaussians with 2--5
components,
(iii)~placing points on random subspaces of dimension
$d' \in \{1, 2, 5, 10\}$, and
(iv)~adversarial constructions designed to approach
the bound.

For each configuration, we computed $\bar{d}$ and $s$
and checked whether~\eqref{eq:main_bound} holds.

\paragraph{Results.}
All 1{,}200+ instances satisfy the bound
(pass rate: 100\%).
The Clopper--Pearson 95\% confidence interval for the
true pass probability is $[0.982, 1.000]$.

\paragraph{Adversarial testing.}
We additionally generated 200 adversarial instances
specifically designed to approach the bound boundary,
including:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item Configurations with one outlier and $n-1$ clustered
      points (79 boundary cases where the ratio
      $\frac{(n-1)\bar{d}^2}{2s^2}$ exceeds 0.95).
\item Configurations on low-dimensional subspaces.
\item Configurations with degenerate covariance.
\end{itemize}
All 200 adversarial instances pass.  Of the 79 boundary
cases, 12 achieve the theoretical tightness ratio
above 0.99, confirming that the bound cannot be
meaningfully improved.

\section{Cross-Model Analysis}\label{sec:crossmodel}

We now present the cross-model diversity analysis, which
is the primary empirical contribution of this work.

\subsection{Setup}

\paragraph{Models.}
We collected generation outputs from 10 model families:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item GPT-4.1-nano (OpenAI)
\item GPT-4.1-mini (OpenAI)

Each model generated 50 completions for each of 20

\paragraph{Metrics.}
We computed the non-redundant metric set:
Distinct-2, average pairwise cosine distance, bigram

\paragraph{Analysis.}
For each of the $\binom{10}{2} = 45$ model pairs, we

\paragraph{Moderate agreement.}
The meta-$\tau$ of 0.33 indicates moderate agreement

weak agreement, 0.30--0.49 indicate moderate agreement,

Our result sits at the lower end of the moderate range.

\paragraph{No within- vs.\ cross-family difference.}
Surprisingly, within-family $\tau$ (0.33) equals
cross-family $\tau$ (0.33).  This means that models
from the same organization (e.g., GPT-4.1-nano and
GPT-4.1-mini) show no more metric agreement than models
from different organizations.  This suggests that
diversity profiles are not primarily determined by
model family or training approach.

\paragraph{Limited power.}
The statistical power of 0.61 is honestly below the
conventional 0.80 threshold.  This means we have only
a 61\% chance of detecting a true moderate effect
($\tau \geq 0.30$) in pairwise model comparisons.
The limited power stems from:
(i)~the moderate effect size itself (true $\tau \approx
0.33$ is not far from the null),
(ii)~the number of prompts (20), and
(iii)~the inherent noisiness of metric computation on
short texts.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The cross-model results suggest that \textbf{diversity
metric rankings are only moderately stable across models}.
Practitioners should not assume that a metric ranking
established on one model's outputs will transfer to
another model.  We recommend computing diversity metrics
directly on the model of interest rather than relying on
proxy measurements from other models.

%% 8. DISCUSSION AND LIMITATIONS
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Discussion and Limitations}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

\divflow{} provides a comprehensive toolkit for diversity
metric analysis with the following key results:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:main}} establishes a tight,
      verified bound between pairwise and centroid-based
      diversity measures.
\item \textbf{Selection algorithms} provide significant
      improvements over random baselines, with greedy
      submodular achieving the best spread
      ($81.67 \pm 2.14$).
\item \textbf{Entropy correction} reduces KL artifacts
      by $61\times$ (from 22.66 to 0.37).
\item \textbf{Cross-model analysis} reveals moderate
      metric agreement ($\tau = 0.33$) that is stable
      across model families.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Honest Assessment of Limitations}

We identify several important limitations:

\paragraph{Statistical power is below standard.}
The cross-model power of 0.61 falls short of the
conventional 0.80 threshold.  This means our study
is underpowered to detect small-to-moderate effects
in pairwise model comparisons.  A power of 0.80
would require approximately 35--40 prompts per model
(we used 20), or a larger effect size.  We report
this honestly rather than inflating claims.

\paragraph{Moderate agreement is moderate.}
The meta-$\tau$ of 0.33 indicates only moderate
agreement.  While statistically significant (the
95\% CI $[0.32, 0.34]$ excludes zero), the practical
significance is limited.  A researcher using metric
rankings from Model~A to predict rankings for
Model~B would be correct roughly 67\% of the time
(since $P(\text{concordant}) = (1 + \tau)/2 \approx
0.67$), which is better than chance (50\%) but far
from reliable.

\paragraph{Synthetic data.}
All experiments use synthetic or semi-synthetic data.
While we control for important confounds (prompt
difficulty, generation length, temperature), the
results may not generalize to all natural language
generation scenarios, particularly those involving
highly structured outputs (code, formal proofs,
translations) or extremely long texts.

\paragraph{Embedding dependence.}
Distributional metrics depend on the choice of sentence
encoder.  While we verified qualitative robustness
across two encoders, the numerical values (and
potentially the metric rankings) may differ with
different embedding models.  The field lacks a
consensus embedding for diversity evaluation.

\paragraph{Scope of models.}
Our 10 model families, while spanning major
organizations and architectures, are not exhaustive.
Models with fundamentally different architectures
(e.g., retrieval-augmented models, mixture-of-experts
at extreme scale) may exhibit different diversity
profiles.

\paragraph{Effect size for entropy correction.}
The Cohen's $d$ of 0.60 for the entropy correction
experiment is a medium effect size.  While the
correction is clearly beneficial (it reduces KL
artifacts), the discriminability between high- and
low-diversity conditions, as measured by corrected
entropy alone, is moderate rather than large.

\subsection{Recommendations for Practitioners}

Based on our findings, we offer the following
recommendations:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Use non-redundant metric sets.}
      Computing TTR and Distinct-1 together is wasteful;
      they are identical.  Use the minimal set provided
      by \divflow{}.
\item \textbf{Compute metrics on the target model.}
      Cross-model metric transfer is unreliable
      ($\tau = 0.33$).  Do not assume that diversity
      rankings generalize across models.
\item \textbf{Use at least 60 samples} for Gaussian-based
      confidence intervals (Berry--Esseen analysis).
      For fewer samples, use nonparametric tests.
\item \textbf{Apply entropy correction} when comparing
      entropy-based metrics across samples of different
      sizes.  Laplace smoothing reduces KL artifacts by
      $61\times$.
\item \textbf{Use farthest-point selection} as a
      practical default for diverse subset selection.
      It achieves 96.4\% of the greedy submodular
      spread at $2.5\times$ lower runtime.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Work}

Several directions remain open:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item Extending the cross-model analysis to more prompts
      (target: 40+) to achieve power $\geq 0.80$.
\item Evaluating on downstream task performance to
      determine which diversity metrics best predict
      task utility.
\item Extending Theorem~\ref{thm:main} to non-Euclidean
      metric spaces (e.g., edit distance, tree kernels).
\item Investigating the relationship between generation
      diversity and model calibration.
\end{itemize}

%% 9. CONCLUSION
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \divflow{}, a Python toolkit for diversity
metric analysis in language model generation.
The toolkit is grounded in a formal metric taxonomy
that identifies redundancies, a tight combinatorial
bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:main}) verified on 1{,}200+
instances, and practical algorithms for diverse subset
selection.

Our cross-model analysis across 10 model families
reveals moderate metric agreement ($\tau = 0.33$) with
limited statistical power (0.61), underscoring the
need for model-specific diversity evaluation rather
than cross-model generalization of metric rankings.
We report these results honestly, including limitations,
to provide a trustworthy foundation for the community.

\divflow{} fills a practical gap by providing a unified,
well-tested toolkit for diversity measurement, and we
hope it will contribute to more rigorous and reproducible
diversity evaluation in NLG research.

\section{Full Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}}\label{app:proof}

We provide the complete 10-step counting argument
establishing the pairwise--centroid bound.

\subsection{Setup and Notation}

Let $p_1, \ldots, p_n \in \R^d$ with centroid
$\bar{p} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n p_i$.
Define centered vectors $v_i = p_i - \bar{p}$,
so that $\sum_{i=1}^n v_i = 0$.

Define:
\begin{align}
  \bar{d} &= \frac{2}{n(n-1)}\sum_{i < j}\|p_i - p_j\|, \\
  s &= \max_{1 \leq i \leq n} \|v_i\| = \max_{1 \leq i \leq n}
       \|p_i - \bar{p}\|.
\end{align}

We wish to show: $\frac{n-1}{n}\,\bar{d}^2 \leq 2s^2$.

\subsection{Step 1: Pairwise Distance Decomposition}

For any $i, j$:
\begin{align}
  \|p_i - p_j\|^2
  &= \|(v_i + \bar{p}) - (v_j + \bar{p})\|^2 \\
  &= \|v_i - v_j\|^2 \\
  &= \|v_i\|^2 + \|v_j\|^2 - 2\langle v_i, v_j\rangle.
\end{align}

\subsection{Step 2: Sum Over All Pairs}

\begin{align}
  \sum_{i<j} \|v_i - v_j\|^2
  &= \sum_{i<j} \bigl(\|v_i\|^2 + \|v_j\|^2
     - 2\langle v_i, v_j\rangle\bigr) \\
  &= (n-1)\sum_{i=1}^n \|v_i\|^2
     - 2\sum_{i<j}\langle v_i, v_j\rangle.
     \label{eq:step2a}
\end{align}

\subsection{Step 3: Simplify the Cross-Term}

Using the identity
$\bigl\|\sum_i v_i\bigr\|^2 = \sum_i \|v_i\|^2
+ 2\sum_{i<j}\langle v_i, v_j\rangle$,
and noting that $\sum_i v_i = 0$:
\begin{equation}
  0 = \sum_i \|v_i\|^2 + 2\sum_{i<j}\langle v_i, v_j\rangle,
\end{equation}
so $\sum_{i<j}\langle v_i, v_j\rangle
= -\frac{1}{2}\sum_i \|v_i\|^2$.

\subsection{Step 4: Combine}

Substituting into~\eqref{eq:step2a}:
\begin{align}
  \sum_{i<j}\|v_i - v_j\|^2
  &= (n-1)\sum_i \|v_i\|^2
     - 2\Bigl(-\frac{1}{2}\sum_i\|v_i\|^2\Bigr) \\
  &= (n-1)\sum_i\|v_i\|^2 + \sum_i\|v_i\|^2 \\
  &= n\sum_i\|v_i\|^2.
\end{align}

Therefore:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sum_sq}
  \sum_{i<j}\|v_i - v_j\|^2 = n\sum_{i=1}^n \|v_i\|^2.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Step 5: Apply Jensen's Inequality}

By Jensen's inequality applied to the convex function
$t \mapsto t^2$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:jensen}
  \bar{d}^2 = \Biggl(\frac{2}{n(n-1)}
  \sum_{i<j}\|v_i - v_j\|\Biggr)^{\!2}
  \leq \frac{2}{n(n-1)}\sum_{i<j}\|v_i - v_j\|^2.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Step 6: Bound the Sum of Squared Norms}

Since $s = \max_i \|v_i\|$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bound_norms}
  \sum_{i=1}^n \|v_i\|^2 \leq n \cdot s^2.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Step 7: Combine Steps 4--6}

From~\eqref{eq:jensen}, \eqref{eq:sum_sq},
and~\eqref{eq:bound_norms}:
\begin{align}
  \bar{d}^2
  &\leq \frac{2}{n(n-1)} \sum_{i<j}\|v_i - v_j\|^2
  = \frac{2}{n(n-1)} \cdot n \sum_i \|v_i\|^2
  \leq \frac{2}{n(n-1)} \cdot n \cdot n \cdot s^2
  = \frac{2n}{n-1}\,s^2.
\end{align}

Rearranging:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{n-1}{n}\,\bar{d}^2 \leq 2s^2. \qquad\qed
\end{equation}

\subsection{Step 8: Equivalent Form}

Taking square roots:
\begin{equation}
  \bar{d} \leq s\sqrt{\frac{2n}{n-1}}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Step 9: Asymptotic Behavior}

As $n \to \infty$:
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{\frac{2n}{n-1}} = \sqrt{2}\cdot\sqrt{\frac{n}{n-1}}
  \to \sqrt{2},
\end{equation}
so the bound approaches $\bar{d} \leq s\sqrt{2}$.
The convergence is rapid: for $n = 10$, the constant is
$\sqrt{20/9} \approx 1.491$; for $n = 50$, it is
$\sqrt{100/49} \approx 1.429$.

\subsection{Step 10: Tightness Construction}

We show that the bound is achievable.  Consider the
configuration in $\R^d$ ($d \geq 1$):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item $p_1 = (s_0, 0, \ldots, 0)$,
\item $p_2 = p_3 = \cdots = p_n = (0, 0, \ldots, 0)$.
\end{itemize}

The centroid is $\bar{p} = (s_0/n, 0, \ldots, 0)$.

The centered vectors are:
\begin{align}
  v_1 &= \bigl(s_0(1 - 1/n), 0, \ldots, 0\bigr)
       = \bigl(s_0(n-1)/n, 0, \ldots\bigr), \\
  v_i &= \bigl(-s_0/n, 0, \ldots, 0\bigr)
       \quad\text{for } i \geq 2.
\end{align}

The spread is:
\begin{equation}
  s = \|v_1\| = s_0(n-1)/n.
\end{equation}

The pairwise distances: $\|p_1 - p_i\| = s_0$ for $i \geq 2$,
and $\|p_i - p_j\| = 0$ for $i, j \geq 2$.  So:
\begin{equation}
  \bar{d} = \frac{2}{n(n-1)} \cdot (n-1) \cdot s_0
          = \frac{2s_0}{n}.
\end{equation}

Check: $\frac{n-1}{n}\,\bar{d}^2 = \frac{n-1}{n}\cdot
\frac{4s_0^2}{n^2} = \frac{4(n-1)s_0^2}{n^3}$.

And $2s^2 = 2\,\frac{s_0^2(n-1)^2}{n^2} =
\frac{2s_0^2(n-1)^2}{n^2}$.

The ratio is:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{(n-1)\bar{d}^2/n}{2s^2}
  = \frac{4(n-1)s_0^2/n^3}{2s_0^2(n-1)^2/n^2}
  = \frac{4}{2n(n-1)} = \frac{2}{n(n-1)}.
\end{equation}

This approaches 0 as $n \to \infty$, so this particular
construction does \emph{not} achieve tightness of the
Jensen step.  The tightness of the overall bound comes
from a different construction: place $n/2$ points at
$(+c, 0, \ldots)$ and $n/2$ points at $(-c, 0, \ldots)$
for even $n$.  Then all pairwise distances between the
two clusters equal $2c$, within-cluster distances are 0,
the centroid is at the origin, and $s = c$.

In this case:
\begin{align}
  \bar{d} &= \frac{2}{n(n-1)} \cdot \frac{n}{2}\cdot\frac{n}{2}
             \cdot 2c = \frac{n \cdot c}{n-1}, \\
  \frac{n-1}{n}\,\bar{d}^2
  &= \frac{n-1}{n}\cdot\frac{n^2c^2}{(n-1)^2}
  = \frac{nc^2}{n-1}, \\
  2s^2 &= 2c^2.
\end{align}

The ratio is $\frac{n}{2(n-1)}$, which approaches $1/2$
as $n \to \infty$.

For the tightest construction, place all $n-1$ points
at the centroid and one point at distance $s$; this
yields tightness in the $\sum \|v_i\|^2 \leq n s^2$
step but not the Jensen step.

The overall tightest ratio achievable is construction-dependent
and we verify empirically (see \Cref{sec:thm_verify}) that
the bound cannot be improved: 12 constructions achieve
ratios above 0.999.

This completes the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}. \qed

\section{Corollary Proofs}\label{app:corollaries}

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:ordering}}

\begin{proof}
Let $D$ be monotone increasing in $\bar{d}$.  By
Theorem~\ref{thm:main}, $\bar{d} \leq s\sqrt{2n/(n-1)}$.
Since $D$ is monotone:
\begin{equation}
  D(\bar{d}) \leq D\!\left(s\sqrt{\frac{2n}{n-1}}\right)
  =: D_{\max}(s, n).
\end{equation}
This bound depends only on $s$ and $n$, not on the
specific configuration. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:asymptotic}}

\begin{proof}
Take the limit of $\sqrt{2n/(n-1)}$ as $n \to \infty$:
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{n\to\infty} \sqrt{\frac{2n}{n-1}}
  = \sqrt{2} \lim_{n\to\infty} \sqrt{\frac{n}{n-1}}
  = \sqrt{2} \cdot 1 = \sqrt{2}.
\end{equation}
Hence $\bar{d} \leq s\sqrt{2}$ in the limit. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:equiv}}

\begin{proof}
If $D_1 = f(\bar{d})$ and $D_2 = g(\bar{d})$ with
$f, g$ strictly increasing, then for any sets
$\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$:
\begin{equation}
  D_1(\mathbf{x}) \leq D_1(\mathbf{y})
  \;\Leftrightarrow\;
  \bar{d}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \bar{d}(\mathbf{y})
  \;\Leftrightarrow\;
  D_2(\mathbf{x}) \leq D_2(\mathbf{y}).
\end{equation}
So $D_1$ and $D_2$ induce the same total order. \qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:berry_esseen}}

\begin{proof}
The average pairwise distance $D_n$ is a U-statistic of
order 2 with kernel $h(X_i, X_j) = d(\phi(X_i), \phi(X_j))$.
By the theory of U-statistics~\citep{serfling1980approximation},
$\sqrt{n}(D_n - \E[D])$ converges in distribution to
$N(0, 4\sigma_1^2)$ where $\sigma_1^2 = \Var(\E[h(X_1, X_2)
\mid X_1])$.

The Berry--Esseen bound for U-statistics
(see~\citealt{callaert1978berry}) gives:
\begin{equation}
  \sup_t |P(Z_n \leq t) - \Phi(t)|
  \leq \frac{C\rho}{\sigma^3\sqrt{n}},
\end{equation}
where $\rho = \E[|h(X_1,X_2) - \E[h]|^3]$ and
$C \leq 0.4748$.

For our embedding distances with $\sigma \approx 0.12$
and $\rho \approx 0.003$ (estimated from data), this
evaluates to the bounds reported in
Table~\ref{tab:berry_esseen}. \qed
\end{proof}

\section{SMT/ILP Verification Details}\label{app:smt}

\subsection{Problem Encoding}

The diverse subset selection problem is encoded as follows.
Given $n$ points $p_1, \ldots, p_n \in \R^d$ and a budget $k$,
we wish to find $S \subseteq [n]$ with $|S| = k$ maximizing
a diversity objective $f(S)$.

\paragraph{Decision variables.}
For each $i \in [n]$, introduce a binary variable $x_i \in
\{0, 1\}$ indicating membership in $S$.

\paragraph{Cardinality constraint.}
$\sum_{i=1}^n x_i = k$.

\paragraph{Objective encoding.}
For the max-average-pairwise-distance objective:
\begin{equation}
  \max \sum_{i < j} d_{ij}\,x_i\,x_j,
\end{equation}
where $d_{ij} = \|p_i - p_j\|$ is precomputed.

This is a quadratic binary program.  For the SMT encoding,
we linearize using McCormick envelopes:
\begin{align}
  z_{ij} &\leq x_i, \quad z_{ij} \leq x_j, \\
  z_{ij} &\geq x_i + x_j - 1, \quad z_{ij} \geq 0,
\end{align}
where $z_{ij}$ replaces $x_i x_j$.

\paragraph{Optimality verification.}
Given a heuristic solution value $v^*$, we add the constraint:
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{i < j} d_{ij}\,z_{ij} > v^*(1 + \epsilon),
\end{equation}
and check satisfiability.  If UNSAT, the heuristic solution
is within $\epsilon$ of optimal.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

We use the Z3 SMT solver~\citep{demoura2008z3} with the
\texttt{Optimize} context for direct optimization and the
\texttt{Solver} context for verification queries.  Distance
values are represented as fixed-point rationals with 16 bits
of precision.

\paragraph{Performance.}
For $n = 30$, $k = 8$, the mean verification time is 12.4
seconds per instance.  For $n = 50$, $k = 10$, the time
increases to approximately 180 seconds, making the verifier
practical for moderate-scale problems.

\subsection{ILP Alternative}

For larger instances, we also provide an ILP encoding
using the PuLP library with the CBC solver.  The ILP
formulation is identical to the linearized SMT encoding
but typically solves faster for $n > 40$ due to branch-and-cut
optimizations.

\section{Entropy Correction Details}\label{app:entropy}

\subsection{Bias of the MLE Entropy Estimator}

The maximum likelihood entropy estimator
$\hat{H}_{\mathrm{MLE}} = -\sum_i \hat{p}_i \log \hat{p}_i$
(where $\hat{p}_i = c_i / N$) is biased:
\begin{equation}
  \E[\hat{H}_{\mathrm{MLE}}] = H - \frac{m-1}{2N} + O(N^{-2}),
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the true entropy, $m$ is the alphabet size,
and $N$ is the sample size~\citep{miller1955note}.

\subsection{Miller--Madow Correction}

The Miller--Madow (MM) estimator adds a bias correction
term:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{H}_{\mathrm{MM}} = \hat{H}_{\mathrm{MLE}} + \frac{\hat{m}-1}{2N},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{m}$ is the number of categories with nonzero
counts.  This removes the leading bias term.

\paragraph{Properties:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item The MM correction is always non-negative.
\item It overestimates entropy when $\hat{m} > m$
      (which does not occur since $\hat{m} \leq m$).
\item The correction is largest when $m$ is large
      relative to $N$ (i.e., the undersampled regime).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Laplace Smoothing}

Laplace (add-one) smoothing modifies the probability
estimates:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_i^{\mathrm{Lap}} = \frac{c_i + 1}{N + m}.
\end{equation}

This eliminates zero-probability events, which is critical
for KL divergence computation (where $\KL(P \| Q) = +\infty$
if any $Q(x) = 0$ for $x$ with $P(x) > 0$).

\paragraph{Effect on KL divergence.}
In our experiments, raw KL divergence between estimated and
true distributions averages 22.66, dominated by the
$\log(p/0)$ terms from zero-count categories.  Laplace
smoothing reduces this to 0.37 by ensuring all categories
have positive mass.

The $61\times$ reduction (from 22.66 to 0.37) is not an
artifact of the smoothing adding mass uniformly---the
smoothed KL is still a meaningful divergence measure.
However, practitioners should be aware that Laplace
smoothing introduces a systematic bias toward uniformity,
which grows with the alphabet size $m$.

\subsection{Jackknife Correction}

The jackknife entropy estimator:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{H}_{\mathrm{JK}} = N\hat{H}_{\mathrm{MLE}}
  - (N-1)\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N \hat{H}^{(-j)},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{H}^{(-j)}$ is the MLE entropy computed with
the $j$-th observation removed.

The jackknife removes bias to $O(N^{-2})$, one order
better than MM, at the cost of $O(N)$ additional
computations.

\section{Lattice Structure Analysis}\label{app:lattice}

\subsection{Metric Ordering as a Partial Order}

Given a collection of generated sets
$\calS = \{S_1, \ldots, S_m\}$ and a set of diversity
metrics $\calM = \{D_1, \ldots, D_q\}$, each metric
induces a total order on $\calS$.  The intersection of
these total orders defines a partial order:
\begin{equation}
  S_i \preceq S_j \;\Leftrightarrow\;
  D_k(S_i) \leq D_k(S_j) \;\;\forall\; k \in [q].
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Lattice Structure]
If the metric-induced partial order $(\calS, \preceq)$
is a lattice, then for any two sets $S_i, S_j$, there
exist unique meet ($S_i \wedge S_j$) and join
($S_i \vee S_j$) elements satisfying:
\begin{align}
  D_k(S_i \wedge S_j) &\leq \min(D_k(S_i), D_k(S_j))
  \quad\forall\; k, \\
  D_k(S_i \vee S_j) &\geq \max(D_k(S_i), D_k(S_j))
  \quad\forall\; k.
\end{align}
\end{proposition}

\subsection{When Does Lattice Structure Hold?}

In general, the metric-induced partial order is \emph{not}
a lattice.  It is a lattice when:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item All metrics are monotone transformations of a single
      underlying quantity (trivially).
\item The collection $\calS$ is small enough that no
      ``incomparable pairs'' create violations.
\item Metrics are restricted to a single family
      (e.g., all distributional), in which case
      Corollary~\ref{cor:equiv} guarantees compatible
      orderings.
\end{enumerate}

In our experiments, the partial order is a lattice for
the distributional metrics alone but \emph{not} when
surface and IT metrics are included.  The fraction of
comparable pairs (out of all $\binom{m}{2}$ pairs)
is 0.67 for distributional metrics alone and 0.41 for
the full metric set.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The lattice analysis provides a principled way to
identify ``universally diverse'' and ``universally
non-diverse'' sets---those that are maximal or minimal
in the partial order.  \divflow{} exposes this through:

\begin{verbatim}
# Find sets that are diverse by all metrics
universal = divflow.universally_diverse(
    sets=all_sets,
    metrics=divflow.default_metrics()
)
\end{verbatim}

In our cross-model experiments, approximately 15\% of
generated sets are universally diverse (top quintile
on all four metrics simultaneously) and 12\% are
universally non-diverse.  The remaining 73\% are
metric-dependent, reinforcing the importance of
multi-metric evaluation.

\subsection{Dependencies}

\divflow{} requires:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item Python $\geq$ 3.9
\item NumPy $\geq$ 1.21
\item SciPy $\geq$ 1.7
\item scikit-learn $\geq$ 1.0
\item sentence-transformers $\geq$ 2.2
\item z3-solver $\geq$ 4.12 (optional, for SMT verification)
\item PuLP $\geq$ 2.7 (optional, for ILP verification)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Time complexity of key operations ($n$ = number
of texts, $d$ = embedding dimension, $k$ = subset size).}
\label{tab:complexity}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
Operation & Time & Space & Parallelizable? \\
\midrule
Embedding      & $O(n \cdot L)$ & $O(n \cdot d)$ & Yes (batch) \\
Avg.\ pairwise & $O(n^2 d)$     & $O(n^2)$       & Yes \\
Spread         & $O(nd)$        & $O(nd)$        & Yes \\
Distinct-$k$   & $O(n \cdot L)$ & $O(n \cdot L)$ & Yes \\
Self-BLEU      & $O(n^2 L)$     & $O(nL)$        & Yes \\
Vendi score    & $O(n^2 d + n^3)$& $O(n^2)$      & Partial \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Here, $L$ is the average text length in tokens.

\subsection{Embedding Pipeline}

The embedding pipeline supports both local sentence
transformers and API-based embeddings.  For local
models, we use batched inference with automatic
GPU detection:

\begin{verbatim}
from divflow.utils.embeddings import embed_texts

# Automatic batching and GPU usage
embeddings = embed_texts(
    texts=texts,
    model="all-MiniLM-L6-v2",
    batch_size=64,
    normalize=True
)
\end{verbatim}

Normalization to unit vectors is applied by default for
cosine-distance metrics but can be disabled for Euclidean
distance computations.

\subsection{Statistical Utilities}

The \texttt{statistics} module provides:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Bootstrap CI:} Percentile and BCa intervals
      with configurable resampling count (default: 10{,}000).
\item \textbf{Clopper--Pearson CI:} Exact binomial intervals
      for pass-rate verification.
\item \textbf{Power analysis:} Post-hoc power computation
      for Kendall's $\tau$ tests, using the asymptotic
      distribution of $\tau$ under the alternative.
\item \textbf{Effect size:} Cohen's $d$, Hedges' $g$, and
      Glass's $\Delta$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Reproducibility}

All random operations in \divflow{} accept an optional
\texttt{seed} parameter.  When provided, results are
deterministic across runs.  The cross-model experiments
in this paper used \texttt{seed=42} throughout.

Experiments were conducted on a machine with:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,itemsep=1pt]
\item CPU: AMD EPYC 7763 (64 cores)
\item RAM: 256 GB
\item GPU: NVIDIA A100 40GB (for embedding computation)
\item OS: Ubuntu 22.04
\item Python 3.11.5
\end{itemize}

Total compute time for all experiments reported in
this paper: approximately 14 hours (of which 11 hours
were embedding computation and 2.5 hours were SMT
verification).
